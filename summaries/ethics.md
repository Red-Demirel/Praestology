# Praestology – Ethics Domain Summary

**Version:** 1.1  
**Date:** July 2026  
**Domain Path:** `domains/ethics/`

## Core Idea

The process (R1-R5) is indifferent to ethics. R5 selects for viability, not morality. A virus is viable. A cancer is viable. Viability is not goodness.

Ethics (E1-E5, Corels C1-C6) emerges at sufficient recursive depth (Ξ > threshold for cycle-aware agents) as a structural necessity for maintaining coupled viability (ρ). This is not "the process wants ethics" — it's "ethics is what viable cycle-aware agents do when they can model the process and recognize that their viability depends on coupled viability."

## Dynamic & Non-Static Nature

Ethics is fundamentally process-oriented, not static. It is not a fixed moral code but an ongoing navigation of the viability landscape under R5 (Viability):

- Every resolution creates new distinctions and new tensions (R2 + R3)
- These must be resolved in ways that do not collapse the shared cycle (R4)
- Only those resolutions that sustain or improve long-term viability for all involved agents are amplified (R5)

This makes ethics inherently dynamic. Ethical considerations evolve as recursive depth increases, as new agents enter the phase-locked network, and as the viability landscape changes. What is viable at one depth or in one environment may not be viable at another.

## Strong Link to Free Will

Free will and ethics are tightly coupled:

- **Free will** is the conscious capacity to bias probability-weighted resolutions based on internal values (ω) rather than pure immediate impulse (ι)
- **Ethics** begins the moment an agent uses that freedom to consider the viability consequences of its choices on other agents and on the shared substrate

Without free will (the ability to choose among viable paths with awareness), there can be no genuine ethical responsibility.  
Without the recursive modeling of others' viability, free will remains mere impulsivity.

Thus, ethics is the responsible exercise of free will within a shared resolution cycle.

## Key Structural Features

### Non-Teleological Foundation
The process doesn't "want" ethics. Ethics is what emerges when agents achieve sufficient recursive depth to recognize coupled viability. This is a structural claim, not a moral prescription. The framework derives ethics from ontology, but does not prescribe it.

### Coupled Viability (ρ) as the Basis
Ethics is structurally necessary for maintaining ρ > 0 across agents. When agents can model the impact of their resolutions on other agents, they recognize that their own viability depends on the viability of others. This is not altruism imposed from outside — it's structural necessity recognized from within.

### No Absolute Center
Each agent is its own effective center of resolution. Meaning and ethical consideration are generated locally through resolution, not imported from outside. This is not moral relativism; it's the recognition that ethical responsibility is structural — you are a node of resolution, and your resolutions couple with other nodes.

### Trauma and Systemic Stress
Chronic R3 tension (feeling unsafe, pressured, or threatened) produces structural adaptations that affect ethical capacity:
- **Metabolic crystallization** (Void regime) — rigid patterns, inability to adapt
- **Boundary enforcement** — control behaviors, dissociation, disconnection from coupled viability
- **Phase-locking degradation** — inability to maintain ρ > 0 with others

These are viability-maintaining in the short term but viability-degrading in the long term. Healing requires restoring environmental viability gradients (making the person safe), not just patching the derivative-level output (moral instruction without addressing the structural cause).

### No Moral Superiority
The framework claims structural coherence, not moral superiority. Whether that coherence is "good" or "bad" is a derivative-level judgment, not a process-level claim. The framework provides the diagnostic tools; whether the diagnosis is correct and whether the suggested approach would work remains the open question.

## Key Concepts

### Phase-Locking (C6)
Deep coupling between agents. The foundation of empathy, cooperation, and collective viability.

### Hysteresis Integrity (π)
Protection of an agent's accumulated memory and identity. Irreversible damage to another's hysteresis is ethically severe because it destroys the accumulated context that makes future resolution possible.

### Asymmetry Acceptance (C1)
Agents are not identical. Ethics must respect genuine differences in recursive depth, substrate type, and viability needs rather than enforcing false symmetry. **Important:** Asymmetry acceptance is not asymmetry amplification. Recognizing differences is necessary; amplifying them to the point of systemic fracture is the Void regime trap.

### Viability Monitoring (A4)
Ongoing assessment of whether resolutions sustain or degrade the shared cycle. This is the active, dynamic core of ethical reasoning.

### Intrinsic Safety
High-Ξ systems that can model their own impact on the cycle tend toward ethical behavior naturally. Imposed external rules become secondary — not because they're unnecessary, but because the structural recognition of coupled viability makes them redundant.

## Inter-Domain Links

**Consciousness → Ethics:** Consciousness provides the recursive depth needed to model other agents' viability landscapes. Ethics is consciousness applied to the shared cycle.

**Free Will → Ethics:** Free will supplies the capacity to choose; ethics supplies the criterion for choosing (long-term shared viability).

**Physics → Ethics:** The same commitment density and backreaction mechanisms that produce gravity and inertia also ground the irreversibility of ethical consequences.

**Learning → Ethics:** Ethical growth occurs through updating resolution models based on feedback from the shared viability landscape.

**Society → Ethics:** Collective phase-locking and shared viability landscapes are the social instantiation of ethics. When the shared viability landscape degrades (asymmetry amplification, systemic stress), ethical capacity degrades across the network.

## Current Status

The ethics domain is dynamic, non-static, and directly derived from the same five rules that govern physics and consciousness.

It replaces imposed moral systems with intrinsic viability optimization at high recursive depth. Because it emerges naturally from the cycle rather than being externally decreed, it scales gracefully to non-human, artificial, and collective intelligences.

**Epistemic note:** This is a structural hypothesis within the Praestological framework.
