# Why Every Analogy Here Eventually Breaks

*A standalone clarification, referenced from other Praestology texts rather than repeated inside them.*

**Location:** `/core/analogy-disclaimer.md`  
**Status:** Permanent reference — does not modify any specification  
**How to reference:** Add `see_also: ["../core/analogy-disclaimer.md"]` to any file  
where a metaphor or analogy is introduced, or where a reader might mistake a  
pointer-word (fluid, wave, flow, field, process) for a literal description.  
Do not embed the content — link to this file.

## The Problem in One Sentence

Every word we have was minted downstream, to describe things that already exist
inside a world that has already differentiated space, substance, time, and motion.
When you reach for one of those words to describe the process that generates space,
substance, time, and motion in the first place, you're borrowing a structure that
came *after* the thing you're trying to point at — and that structure carries hidden
assumptions that don't apply upstream.

Take "fluid." The word calls up water in a riverbed: a substance, moving through a
container, made of molecules that already exist, in a space that already exists for
it to move through. That's a perfectly good picture of how *liquids* behave. It's a
misleading picture of Praesto, the field this framework starts from — because Praesto
isn't a substance occupying space. There's no container, no pre-existing space for it
to be "in," and nothing more basic underneath it that it's made of. The fluid-dynamics
equations are the right *mathematical* description of how it behaves — but the moment
you picture a fluid, you've quietly imported a container that doesn't exist here.

"Wave" has the same problem. A wave needs a medium to travel through and a direction
to travel in. "Flow" needs a path already laid out. Even "process" — a word we use
constantly in this framework — comes from human experience of *sequential* activity
happening *inside time*. But time, here, is supposed to be a downstream feature of the
process, not something it needs in advance to operate.

There is no way around this. There is no analogy available to us that doesn't have
this problem, because there is nothing else to build analogies *from* except the
downstream, already-differentiated world we live in. Every metaphor is a
derivative-level word pointing backward at the process-level thing that generated
derivative-level words to begin with.

## This Includes "The Wave-Function"

It's worth being honest that this same problem applies to ψ itself — the term we use
for the representation of Praesto's ongoing dynamics.

"Wave-function" is a term born inside quantum mechanics — itself a realized-level
theory, a description of committed, measurable behavior. Repurposing it to point at
something *prior to* and *generative of* quantum mechanics carries the same risk as
"fluid": picture an actual wave, and you've smuggled in a medium for it to ripple
through, when there's no such pre-existing medium here. ψ is a useful representation
of the dynamics. It is not a literal wave any more than Praesto is a literal fluid.

Naming this here, once, matters more than catching it case by case. Every term in
this framework — fluid, wave, flow, field, process, even "loop" — should be read as
a **pointer, not a picture.** The mathematics is precise even where the words
available to describe it in plain language are not. This isn't a new problem invented
for Praestology. Physics already lives with it: an electron doesn't literally spin,
and a quark has no actual color. "Spin" and "color charge" are pointers too — names
borrowed for something structurally real but pictorially false. Praestology just needs
to say this once, plainly, instead of re-explaining it every time a new metaphor
shows up.

## The One Exception: Geometric Algebra

There's a reasonable worry that this same problem applies to **geometric algebra (GA)**
— after all, "geometric" suggests shapes, and shapes are about as realized-level as it
gets. A few of the people we've talked to about this felt the same unease they felt
about "fluid."

It's worth being precise about why that worry doesn't actually transfer.

Fluid, wave, and flow are words for *specific physical phenomena* — they describe how
a particular kind of stuff behaves, and they carry that stuff's prerequisites along
with them. Geometry is different in kind. We're using "geometry" the way it's always
fundamentally meant: **a means of describing relationship, structure, and orientation**
— not a claim about pictures, shapes, or spatial extension existing prior to anything
else. Geometric algebra is a mathematical *language*, not a physical *substance*. It
doesn't claim Praesto is made of triangles, or that it lives in a literal space with
literal angles. It claims that the *relationships* between commitment, orientation, and
rotation — concepts that have to exist at some level for anything structured to emerge
at all — are best expressed using grade, rotors, and bivectors, rather than the more
cumbersome and less natural toolkit of separate vectors and matrices physics has used
for the last century.

This is the distinction worth holding onto: **fluid, wave, and flow are analogies
borrowed from downstream phenomena. Geometric algebra is a notation for describing
relationship itself, at whatever level relationship exists.** The first kind of
borrowing smuggles in unearned assumptions (a container, a medium, a path). The second
kind doesn't, because relationship and orientation aren't downstream phenomena waiting
to be borrowed from — they're as basic as anything gets. Calling the language
"geometric" is no more misleading than calling logic "logical."

That said, geometric algebra is still primarily known to most people as an extension
of vector algebra — a tool for describing things in ordinary 3D space. Worth being
clear: when applied to Praesto, the *grades* (scalar, vector, bivector, trivector) are
not claims about literal spatial dimensions. They are a way of tracking how committed
or open a configuration is — grade reduction is commitment, not "movement through
space." The notation is doing structural work, not spatial work. That's the sense in
which it earns an exception the other metaphors don't.

## What This Means for Reading Praestology

Every picture in these posts — the river, the dam, the vortex, the wave — is doing
honest work as a *pointer toward* something, never as a literal description of what
that something *is*. When a metaphor starts to feel like it's straining, that's not a
sign the framework is wrong. It's a sign you've reached the edge of what any borrowed,
downstream word can do, and it's time to let the mathematics carry the weight the
words can't.

This is true of every analogy used so far, and it will be true of every analogy used
from here on. Better metaphors won't fix it. Only remembering what metaphors are for
will.

## A Note for Framework Editors

The word "derivative" appears in several core files
(`derivative-reality-clarification.yaml`, `process-to-observable-hierarchy.yaml`)
as a *conceptual level-label* — "derivative-level physics," "derivative-level
description." This usage is a technical term in the framework, not a mathematical
claim about differentiation. The mathematical relationship between ψ and η is
integration (η = ∫R(ψ)dτ), not differentiation. The level-label and the
mathematical relationship are two different things; do not conflate them.

This is itself an instance of the broader problem this document describes:
"derivative" was borrowed from mathematics to label a conceptual level, and
it carries the mathematical meaning along with it wherever it goes. The label
is retained because it's established in the framework; the mathematical
correction is documented in `derivative-reality-clarification.yaml`.
